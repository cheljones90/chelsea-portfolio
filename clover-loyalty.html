Problem & Background

A loyalty product that merchants weren't using and customers barely knew existed.

The Situation

Clover acquired Perca to bring loyalty into its POS ecosystem, giving merchants a way to reward repeat customers without leaving the platform. The intention was right. The execution needed work.

Adoption was low across the board. Merchants struggled with an unguided setup experience that made creating a loyalty program feel harder than it should. Customers had little visibility into rewards they were earning. And a flat-rate pricing model meant Clover was leaving meaningful revenue on the table regardless of how merchants used the product.

The brief: Reimagine the loyalty experience end-to-end. Make it intuitive for merchants to set up and manage. Make it visible and rewarding for customers. And find the design opportunities that could unlock better monetization through smarter feature architecture.

Two Audiences

Merchants

Set it up. Actually use it.

Business owners needed a clearer path from signing up for loyalty to running a program their customers would notice. Onboarding, program creation, and a dashboard to track and manage rewards were all broken or missing.

Customers

Know you're earning. Want to come back.

Shoppers had no meaningful touchpoint with the loyalty program during checkout. Signing up was unclear, points accumulation was invisible, and there was nothing to nudge someone to join before they walked out the door.

What Merchants Told Us

The five merchant interviews confirmed what the competitive analysis suggested. Merchants wanted to run loyalty programs but found the setup process confusing and time-consuming. Several weren't using the feature at all because they couldn't figure out where to start. Those who had set it up weren't confident their customers knew the program existed.

The absence of customer interviews was a real constraint. Clover's infrastructure made direct customer access difficult to facilitate. We used merchant feedback as our proxy, supplemented by behavioral patterns from competitor research, to build a picture of what the customer-side experience needed to do.

Recommendations

From the research we produced a feature recommendation document for Clover stakeholders, a prioritised list of improvements grounded in best-in-class patterns, covering both the merchant and customer sides of the experience. This also included the tiered pricing conversation: moving away from flat-rate to a model where features unlock at different tiers, creating both a better merchant experience and a clearer path to revenue growth.

Scope of Design

The work spanned both sides of the Clover experience across the full device suite, from the merchant-facing web dashboard to the customer-facing POS screens.

On the merchant side: loyalty and marketing setup and onboarding, program creation, and a dashboard for viewing and managing rewards. On the customer side: signing up for loyalty, post-payment points accumulation, and checkout speed bumps to prompt sign-up before a transaction ended.

The Hard Part

The client wanted to move backwards. The goal was to keep moving forward.

The Constraint

Clover's infrastructure wasn't ready for the level of innovation the research pointed to. The blue sky ideas, grounded in best-in-class competitor patterns and merchant needs, got scoped down significantly. What started as an ambitious end-to-end reimagining became a more contained MVP.

That's a frustrating place to be in as a designer. You've done the research. You know what users need. And you're being asked to deliver less.

The way through it was to stay focused on what advocacy actually means in practice. You don't always get to build the ideal solution. But you do get to make sure the conversations happen, the ideas are documented, and the door stays open for what comes next. Those conversations with Clover's leadership, including their CEO, planted seeds that are still growing.

The Long Game

There is now a team at Clover actively building out a dedicated loyalty app experience, with plans to integrate it with a marketplace where merchants' customers can shop and redeem rewards. That work traces back directly to the blue sky thinking from this engagement. The win came later, but it wouldn't have been possible without the initial push.

On seeing the designs live in the wild

What I'd Do Differently

I would have found a way to get customer voices into the research earlier, even indirectly. Merchant feedback is valuable, but the customer-side design would have been sharper with more direct signal about what actually drives loyalty behaviour. That's a gap I'd push harder to close if I had the engagement again.
